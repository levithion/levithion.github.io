Problem and product boundary

A frame-level eye classifier is not automatically a drowsiness-detection product. The application must obtain camera data, locate a useful facial region, normalize it for the model, smooth unstable predictions, surface confidence, and create a warning that a driver can notice.

This project explores that complete loop. It is an educational prototype rather than a certified safety device, but it deliberately includes the system components surrounding the classifier instead of presenting only a training notebook.

Model and preprocessing

The repository uses a Keras classifier based on a MobileNetV2 transfer-learning backbone with custom dense layers for binary classification between Drowsy and Alert. MobileNetV2 was chosen because depthwise separable convolutions provide a favourable compute–accuracy trade-off for interactive inference.

  • Face detection: an OpenCV Haar cascade searches the equalized grayscale frame and selects the largest detected face.
  • Eye-region focus: a second cascade looks for up to two large eye regions. If eyes are not found, the pipeline falls back to the upper portion of the face; if no face is found, it uses the full frame.
  • Normalization: the chosen crop is converted to RGB, resized to 224 × 224, scaled to the 0–1 range, and expanded into a model batch.
  • Output interpretation: both scalar sigmoid and two-score model outputs are supported, making the serving utility tolerant of alternative exported heads.

Three inference modes

Uploaded image

The simplest path runs the full preprocessing and prediction pipeline once, returning the class and confidence. This is useful for checking model behaviour and debugging crop selection.

Uploaded video

The video analyzer samples every fifteenth frame, processes at most 120 frames, and aggregates the frame classifications with a majority decision. Sampling controls processing cost and makes the hosted application usable without decoding and inferring on every frame.

Live camera

streamlit-webrtc obtains frames inside the browser. The processor infers every eighth frame, keeps deques of the three most recent alert and drowsy scores, and compares their rolling averages. That short window reduces the chance that a blink or one poor crop flips the interface state.

User feedback and warning path

The interface shows the active crop mode, prediction, and confidence. When the smoothed state becomes drowsy, it renders a prominent warning and plays a generated 800 Hz audio tone. The tone is embedded as base64 audio so it survives Streamlit’s periodic refresh cycle without depending on a separate media file.

Evaluation and caveats

The repository reports 99.99% training accuracy and 100% test accuracy on its train/test split. Those values should be interpreted cautiously: a safety-relevant vision system also needs subject-independent splits, varied camera positions, difficult lighting, glasses and occlusions, demographic coverage, and field testing. Accuracy on a curated image dataset does not prove road readiness.

Haar cascades are lightweight but fragile under extreme pose and lighting. The live hosted demo can also experience limited frame rate because WebRTC plus neural inference competes for free-tier compute. These limitations are stated explicitly rather than hidden behind a single accuracy number.

Next engineering step

Benchmark the full pipeline—not only the CNN—across capture latency, face-detection success, inference time, alert delay, false alarms during normal blinking, and performance by environment.

What the project demonstrated

The central lesson was that temporal and interface design can be as important as the image model. Crop fallbacks, sampling policy, rolling averages, WebRTC behaviour, and warning delivery determine whether a classifier becomes a coherent real-time application.
